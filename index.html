WHAT ARE RARE DISEASES?

WHO defines rare disease as often debilitating lifelong disease or disorder with prevalence of 1 or less, per 1000 population.
  • More than 7000 rare diseases have been identified globally out of which 450 have been reported in India.
  • About 350 million people world wide suffering from rare diseases. If all the people with rarer disease lived in one country, it would be the world third most populous country.
  • Approximately 50% of the people affected by rare diseases are children.
  • Rare diseases are responsible for 35% of death in first year of life.
  • About 30% of children with rare diseases will die before reaching their fifth birthdays.

UNDERLYING PROBLEM
  • Rare diseases are genetic and thus are present throughout the person's entire life, even if symptoms do not immediately appear.
  • More than 7000 rare diseases have been identified globally out of which 450 have been reported in India with only 5% of them having identified treatments.
  • General population lacks awareness about rare diseases.
  • Drugs are mostly not available, and even if they are, they are prohibitively expensive.
  • ROI for research and development (R&D) of a rare disease and its curative drug is not promising.
  • Lack of sufficient funding from government and pharmaceutical industries.
  • Insufficient research leads to poor understanding of the disease mechanism which is a major step towards drug discovery
RARE DISEASE DAY
  • Rare Disease Day is an observance held on the last day of February to raise awareness for rare diseases and improve access to treatment and medical representation for individuals with rare diseases and their families. The European Organisation for Rare Diseases established the day in 2008 to raise awareness for unknown or overlooked illnesses. According to that organization, treatment for many rare diseases is insufficient, as are the social networks to support individuals with rare diseases and their families; furthermore, while there were already numerous days dedicated to individuals with specific diseases (such as AIDS, cancer, etc.), there had previously not been a day for representing those affected by rare diseases. In 2009, Rare Disease Day went global as the National Organization for Rare Disorders mobilized 200 rare disease patient advocacy organizations in the United States while organizations in China, Australia, Taiwan, and Latin America also lead efforts in their respective countries to coordinate activities and promote the day.
